# AtlasPM — Plain English Status Report

Date: March 18, 2026
Scope: Review of the current AtlasPM working tree in `/Users/omri/atlaspm`, including the current dashboards, imports, owner views, public request form, and back-office workflows.

## What the platform can do today

AtlasPM already covers a lot of the day-to-day work a New York City property management team cares about.

### Portfolio and building oversight
- Shows a portfolio overview with occupancy, vacancies, arrears, legal cases, expiring leases, and property-by-property balance data.
- Gives managers a central dashboard for understanding where the biggest problems are across the portfolio.
- Lets teams filter data by building and, in some places, by portfolio.

### Collections and arrears follow-up
- Tracks tenant balances, arrears age, collection notes, follow-up activity, and legal escalation.
- Gives teams a collections dashboard, a tenant-level collections detail page, and an AR report page.
- Supports basic workflows like updating collection status, adding notes, and sending a case to legal.

### Legal tracking
- Tracks active legal cases, court dates, case stage, and legal vendors.
- Connects legal data back to the tenant and building record.
- Surfaces legal counts and legal exposure in dashboards.

### Vacancies, turnovers, and leasing
- Tracks vacant units, days vacant, vacancy stages, asking rent, legal rent, and some access details.
- Supports turnover workflows and vendor assignments.
- Shows vacancy-related metrics like lost rent and longest-vacant units.

### Work orders and operations
- Supports work orders, priority, status, vendors, schedules, and source tracking.
- Includes a public maintenance request form that tenants can use through a tokenized building link.
- Supports bulk updates and vendor management.

### Compliance and violations
- Tracks violations, complaints, compliance deadlines, and certification packets.
- Includes NYC-oriented compliance views and workflows.
- Can create follow-up operational work from compliance issues.

### Projects and capital work
- Tracks projects, milestones, change orders, budgets, approvals, linked violations, and linked work orders.
- Lets management expose selected projects to owners.

### Owner reporting
- Includes an owner dashboard and a simpler owner portal.
- Gives owner-facing views into occupancy, vacancies, arrears totals, legal counts, work orders, and selected projects.
- Hides some detailed tenant-level information for owner users.

### Imports and data setup
- Supports imports for rent rolls, AR aging, legal cases, buildings, legal rents, and staged import review.
- Keeps import logs and some preview/confirmation flows.

### Intelligence and AI-style features
- Includes operational signals, a daily briefing, an AI assistant area, and Themis for work-order draft extraction/promotion.
- Surfaces high-risk items like critical signals, legal pressure, and overdue follow-up.

### Administration
- Supports user management, building assignment, data management, settings, and deploy tooling.

## What works well

### The platform already feels like a real operating system for NYC property management
This is not a toy demo. It already covers arrears, legal, vacancies, work orders, compliance, owner reporting, and imports in one product.

### The strongest value is visibility
AtlasPM does a good job turning a messy portfolio into a short list of what needs attention now: vacant units, overdue balances, legal cases, violations, and urgent maintenance.

### The NYC focus is real
A lot of the product makes sense specifically for New York: violations, legal escalation, owner visibility, rent and vacancy pressure, and day-to-day operational follow-up.

### Owner reporting is better than most early-stage property tools
The owner-facing views are a real strength. Even though they need cleanup, they already point toward a strong commercial story: clear portfolio performance, risk, and active projects.

### The current code passes the basic quality gates
The current codebase passed tests, TypeScript checks, and a production build during this audit. That matters. It means the product is not just visually promising; it is technically coherent enough to package.

## What is incomplete or missing

### It is not a full accounting replacement
AtlasPM does not look ready to replace Yardi or AppFolio as the main accounting system. It works better as an operations and visibility layer on top of them.

### Some workflows still feel half-finished
A few parts of the product look complete on the surface but still rely on loose connections underneath. The biggest examples are vacancies, leases, tenant balances, and collection status. The screens are there, but the underlying data can drift if the workflows are not followed carefully.

### There is still too much product naming for a first client
Argus, Coeus, Themis, Daily Briefing, Owner Dashboard, and Owner Portal all make sense internally, but a client may not immediately understand what each area is for.

### Documentation and onboarding are still thin
There is not much in the repo that would help a new team understand setup, implementation, training, or client rollout. That will matter as soon as a real client asks for a timeline.

### Mobile and field experience are not the product's strongest area yet
The system covers a lot of office workflows, but it does not yet feel like a polished field-operations tool for supers, vendors, and on-site staff.

## What a property manager would notice is broken or confusing

### Some pages are just alternate names that redirect elsewhere
`/work-orders`, `/daily-briefing`, `/owner-view`, `/signals`, and `/turnovers` are mostly alias routes that redirect to the main working pages. That is not a failure, but it does make the product feel less settled.

### Owner reporting is split into two experiences
There is both an Owner Dashboard and an Owner Portal. They overlap. A client would notice that and ask which one is the “real” owner view.

### Collections language is not fully consistent
The collections list, detail screens, signal engine, and legal escalation logic still use more than one status vocabulary. A manager may feel that the product is “mostly right” but not always speaking with one voice.

### Some unit and vacancy details may still be blank or inconsistent
Bedrooms, bathrooms, legal rent, days vacant, and ready dates are supported, but the platform still depends heavily on import quality and disciplined manual updates. A PM would notice if those numbers look incomplete.

### The platform is broad enough that training will matter
Because AtlasPM already does so much, a first-time user will need a guided rollout. Without that, the product could feel powerful but overwhelming.

## Is it ready to show to a real client?

### For a guided demo: yes
AtlasPM is ready to show in a serious demo. It already tells a strong story around NYC operations, owner visibility, arrears, vacancies, legal pressure, and compliance.

### For a pilot with one real client: almost
It is close enough for a controlled pilot if the client understands that this is an operations layer, not the system replacing their accounting software.

### For broad client rollout without hand-holding: not yet
It still needs workflow tightening, naming cleanup, cleaner onboarding, and a little more confidence that the data stays in sync across screens.

## What should be fixed first before showing it widely
- Simplify the product story. Lead with one message: owner visibility and operational control for NYC portfolios.
- Pick one owner-facing experience and make it the clear default.
- Make collections, legal, and vacancy statuses read the same way everywhere.
- Tighten the flows where payments, lease data, and vacancy state can fall out of sync.
- Create a short implementation guide so a client knows what AtlasPM does, what it does not do, and how data gets into the system.

## Bottom line
AtlasPM is already impressive as an internal operating platform and strong enough for a serious demo. Its biggest strength is that it helps a property manager see what is going wrong across a portfolio in one place. Its biggest weakness is that a few underlying workflows still feel more like a powerful in-house system than a polished client-ready product.

My practical assessment: ready to demo, close to pilot, not yet ready for a broad “plug-and-play” rollout without another round of product cleanup.
